Double Jeopardy

Identifying America's most vulnerable communities through five years of air quality data.

Air quality is often dismissed as a temporary inconvenience‚Äîa "smoky day" or a "smog alert." But for millions of Americans, poor air is not an event. It's infrastructure. Using five years of EPA data spanning 2021 to 2025, we mapped a crisis hiding in plain sight: 37 counties where residents face both chronic daily exposure to elevated pollution and acute emergency events that spike air quality into hazardous territory. This analysis provides a roadmap for targeted intervention‚Äîfrom respiratory clinic funding to urban forest deployment.

Understanding Double Jeopardy

We identified two distinct threats to air quality. Chronic exposure is measured by median AQI‚Äîthe baseline air quality residents breathe on a typical Tuesday. This is often tied to permanent factors like industrial corridors or geographic basins that trap pollution. The top 10% threshold for chronic risk is 0 AQI.

Acute events are captured by maximum AQI‚Äîthe single worst day on record. These spikes come from wildfires, industrial accidents, or extreme weather that trigger emergency room visits and school closures. The top 10% threshold for acute risk is 0 AQI.

But the real danger is where they overlap: Double Jeopardy. In these counties, residents' respiratory systems are already compromised by chronic exposure, making them far more vulnerable when acute events strike. This multiplicative effect transforms what might be a manageable "bad air day" elsewhere into a medical emergency.

When AQI exceeds 300, health agencies recommend everyone‚Äînot just sensitive groups‚Äîavoid all outdoor activity. Schools close. Elderly residents face hospitalization. Economic activity halts. These are the counties that have experienced the most severe air quality emergencies over the past five years.

The data reveals a critical distinction: counties with low median but high max AQI can rely on seasonal preparedness (mobile units during fire season). But Double Jeopardy counties need year-round vigilance.

Methodology

Data Sources

This analysis uses EPA Annual Air Quality Index (AQI) data from 2021 through 2025, covering 994 counties across the United States with consistent reporting.

Risk Classification

Counties were classified based on two metrics:

  • Median AQI: Five-year average of median daily AQI (chronic exposure)
  • Max AQI: Five-year average of maximum recorded AQI (acute events)

The top 10% of counties for each metric were identified as "high risk" for that category. Counties appearing in both top 10% groups were classified as "Double Jeopardy."

Limitations

This analysis focuses on air quality metrics and does not account for population density, socioeconomic factors, or health outcomes. The 90th percentile threshold is a statistical convenience; actual health impacts may vary.
